15. Ano ang Holy Roman Empire ?​


Answer:

The Holy Roman Empire ruled over much of western and central Europe from the 9th century to the 19th century. It envisioned itself as a dominion for Christendom continuing in the tradition of the ancient Roman Empire and was characterized by strong papal authority.

Year Event

753

Pope Stephen II anoints Pepin III and his two sons (one of them Charlemagne) in the abbey church of St Denis

771

On the death of his brother, Charlemagne inherits the entire kingdom of the Franks

800

In St Peter's in Rome, on Christmas Day, pope Leo III crowns Charlemagne emperor - supposedly to Charlemagne's surprise

805

Pope Leo III consecrates Charlemagne's new palace chapel in Aachen, modelled on San Vitale in Ravenna

962

The imperial coronation of Otto I by Pope John XII in St Peter's puts in place the formal role of a Holy Roman emperor

c. 1020

Count Radbot builds himself a 'hawk's castle' or Habichstburg, near Zurich, from which the Habsburg dynasty takes its name

1024

Conrad II is elected as the German king, beginning the dynasty variously known as Franconian or Salian

1075

Pope Gregory VII decrees that only the church may make ecclesiastical appointments, thus initiating the investiture controversy between pope and emperor

1077

The emperor Henry IV stands as a penitent outside the pope's castle at Canossa, so as to be released from excommunication.

1138

Conrad III, of the Hohenstaufen family, is elected German king - a title which remains in the family for more than a century, bringing with it that of Holy Roman emperor

c. 1150

In feudal France and Germany Charlemagne is by now venerated as a saint

1152

Frederick Barbarossa becomes king of Germany and Holy Roman emperor, greatly extending the power of the empire during a long reign

1197

The three-year old Frederick II has a claim to the thrones of both Sicily and Germany on the death of his father, the emperor Henry VI

1220

Frederick II is crowned Holy Roman emperor by a somewhat reluctant pope, Honorius III

1254

The death of the last Hohenstaufen ruler, Conrad IV, leaves a vacancy on the German throne which is not filled for nineteen years

1260

The Bohemian prince Otakar II, ruler also of Austria, extends his territories after defeating the Hungarians at Kressenbrunn

1273

The period without a German king, known as the Great Interregnum, ends with the election of a Habsburg prince, Rudolf I

1278

At Dürnkrut Rudolf I defeats and kills Otakar II, his rival for Austria - thus bringing the Austrian territories into the Habsburg domain

1346

Charles IV, king of Bohemia, German king and Holy Roman emperor, makes Prague a glittering centre of learning and architecture

1356

Charles IV establishes a permanent group of seven electors - four hereditary German rulers and the archbishops of Mainz, Cologne and Trier

1438

The office of Holy Roman emperor becomes a hereditary title within the Habsburg dynasty

1477

Maximilian, heir to Austria, weds Mary, heiress to Burgundy, in the first of the great marriage alliances which form the Habsburg empire

1496

Philip, heir to Austria, marries Joanna, a daughter of Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain, in the second of the great Habsburg marital alliances

1516

The death of Ferdinand II results in Spain becoming part of the Habsburg empire, under the rule of Charles V (as Charles I of Spain)

1556

Charles V abdicates, handing the Netherlands and Spain to his son Philip and the title of Holy Roman emperor to his brother Ferdinand

The division by Charles V of his territories means that there are now two Habsburg empires, Austrian and Spanish

1687

The Hungarian diet grants the Habsburg dynasty in Austria a hereditary right to the crown of St Stephen

1781

Joseph II passes an Edict of Toleration, for the first time allowing Protestant worship in Habsburg territories

1806

Francis II formally brings to an end the 1000-year-old Holy Roman Empire, to keep it from the clutches of Napoleon

18. Why was Charlemagne's empire called the "Holy Roman Empire”? ​


Answer:

It was called that because Charlemagne had reunited the majority of the Western Roman Empire under his rule -- an area containing multiple different kingdoms, especially those of the Franks and Lombards. Charlemagne wanted a supranational source of authority and legitimacy that would go beyond his previous status as King of the Franks, and be respected by all his subjects.

He also wanted to associate himself with the prestige of the Roman Empire. He portrayed himself as bringing civilisation back to western Europe after a long period of division and anarchy, and restoring the Roman Empire to its past glory was, symbolically, the best way to do that.

Accordingly, Charlemagne travelled to the city of Rome, which was part of his dominions, and had himself acclaimed and crowned as Emperor using a rite closely modelled on that currently used by the Eastern Roman Emperors in Constantinople. The Byzantine Empire was at first reluctant to give diplomatic recognition to Charlemagne's Empire as co-equal to their own, but eventually did so.

In short, Charlemagne called himself a Roman Emperor because he was crowned using imperial rites in the city of Rome by the Bishop of Rome, ruled over the majority of the Western Roman Empire, and saw himself as a successor to the legacy of Augustus and Constantine.

The description 'Holy' was added to the Empire's name about 350 years after Charlemagne's time. Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa began using it in 1157, as part of his conflict with the Papacy over influence. 'Holy' is actually something of a mistranslation, though one now fixed in popular usage: the Latin word 'sacrum' means something more like 'sacred'. Frederick wasn't making a moral claim that the Empire was good and pure and righteous, but rather that it was a religious institution as well as a secular one. The Emperors ruled by God's grace, and had (he claimed) the right to make laws governing the Church and act as its protector.

Back in Charlemagne's day, it had been clearly understood that the Emperor ruled, and the Pope was merely his most senior bishop. This mirrored the position in the Byzantine Empire where the Patriarch of Constantinople was clearly subject to the dominion of the Basileus. However, the chaos in the West following the decline of the Carolingians allowed successive Popes to claim equality and even superiority to the weak emperors who followed Charlemagne. Frederick Barbarossa calling his state the Holy Empire was one shot in an (ultimately unsuccessful) fight-back.
