20. Full history of the great wall of china


Answer:

Origins

The Great Wall of China started out not as a single, massive wall to protect the people of a unified nation, but rather as several smaller, unconnected walls that were built by warlords fighting each other. During what is known as the "Warring States Period," from 475 B.C. to 221 B.C., China had seven larger states: Chu, Qi, Wei, Yan, Zhao, Qin and Han. To protect their territories, the warlords began building walls for defense.

Unification

It wasn't until Qin Shi Huang became emperor in 220 B.C. that the sections of the wall began to be joined together to form a common defense for China, according to the United Nations. The goal of the unified wall was to defend China against Mongol invaders from the north. With this change in purpose, sections of the wall in central China became useless and were allowed to erode and fall.

Time Frame

The Great Wall of China represents thousands of years of work rather than the massive building project of a single emperor or even a single dynasty. After the Qin Dynasty ended in 206 B.C., construction still continued until 1279, when the Yuan dynasty took control. The Yuan dynasty was a Mongol dynasty that briefly unified the Chinese in the south with the Mongols in the north, rendering the wall unnecessary, so no further work was accomplished. When the Ming Dynasty gained power in 1368, work was restarted and continued until 1644.

Size

The components of the Great Wall of China are more than 5,500 miles long, according to the BBC. The 2009 study by the State Administration of Cultural Heritage and the State Bureau of Surveying and Mapping found that there were almost 3,900 miles of walls, 225 miles of trenches and 1,400 miles of natural barriers to prevent foreign invasion. Before this study, the length of the Great Wall was only estimated to be about 3,100 miles long because sections that had been diminished over time were overlooked.

Tourism

The best time to visit the Great Wall depends on the natural scenery that you prefer and the section of the Great Wall you plan to visit. According to China Highlights, the further north you travel, the colder and rainier the weather will be. Spring trips provide a view of fresh foliage and allow you to avoid the summer tourism season. Summer means larger crowds, but it also provides a view of blooming flowers. July and August bring the most rain. Winter brings the fewest tourists because of the cold temperatures, but the snow on the mountains can provide a stunning view.

History

The Great Wall is traditionally known as the “Long Wall of Ten Thousand Li” to the Chinese. It was built by soldiers, prisoners and local laborers using locally sourced materials including stones, wood, earth and, later, brick. The history of the wall dates back to the Chunqiu era (722 to 481 BC), when construction of walls began during feudal conflicts. Three separate sections of the wall were linked under Qin Shi Huang around 220 BC, forming a cohesive fortification. This wall was expanded under the emperor Wudi between 140 and 87 BC to reach the Bohai Sea in the east. It was intended at that time to prevent invasion by Mongols and Turks. Work on the wall started and stopped periodically until the Ming period of 1368 to 1644 when 5,650 km was added, along with 25,000 towers and 15,000 outposts. Most of the wall remaining today was built during this period. The purpose of the wall was not only to protect China from invasion but also to eliminate foreign influences on Chinese culture.

History

Work began on the Great Wall of China more than 2,000 years ago when Emperor Qin Shi Huang unified China under the Qin dynasty and began to join together walls that were already constructed by previous kingdoms into one great one. The wall, like the smaller walls before it, was built to keep out the invading Huns, who would swoop down from Mongolia and plunder the Chinese villages. During the Middle Ages, the wall was not very effective as a defensive fortification but did serve as an important highway between eastern China and points along the Silk Road and was beneficial for trading. Much of the current wall was built during the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644), again as a defensive measure--this time to stop the Manchu armies from invading and taking China, which it failed to do.

Highlights

Badaling is the most visited section of the wall, and its scenic location, at a mountains pass of more than 3,000 feet in altitude, gives the wall a majestic backdrop despite the hordes of vendors and the weekend crowds. The Huangua section of the Great Wall features wide ramparts, intact parapets and beacon towers while climbing over lush hills with lakes and mountains in the background. The Jinshanling section of the Great Wall in Hebei Province is the best-preserved part of the massive structure and stretches six miles and features 67 watchtowers, each with a legend tied to it. Simatai is a rugged section of wall that lies in Miyun County and features a mountaintop structure called the fairy tower, which must be accessed by a series of steep stone steps called the Stairway to Heaven.

Visiting Independantly

Tourists can easily explore most of the sections that are close to Beijing on their own, as public buses leave the Shanghai for all of the sections close to town on a regular basis. Public buses go directly to Badaling from the old gate of Deshengmen in the center of Beijing and the Dongzhimen long-distance bus station, which can be reached from the city center by bus or taxi, has frequent departures for many of the other major sections.

Tours of the Great Wall

Competition between tour companies is tight, so be sure to bargain. The Beijing Sightseeing Bus Center, located on the side of Tiananmen Sqaure, has a constant queue of tour buses in the daytime that offer several different itineraries, including straight trips to Badaling and back and tours that include side trips to other historic locations such as the Ming Tombs, a Confucian style burial ground that houses the tombs of the last Ming dynasty kings.

Warning

Scamming of tourists is rife in Beijing, and several tour companies that offer trips to the Great Wall may also offer side trips to destinations such as gem factories, where overpriced stones are pushed on tourists or Chinese medicine centers where tourists are diagnosed with strange conditions and offered expensive herbal treatments.

Grasping the History

Explain to your kids that “Great Wall” refers to a series of defensive barriers put up in northern China over the course of 2,200 years, starting in the fifth century B.C. The intent was to stop nomads who lived to the north from invading. It was never a single, long barrier; instead, it’s made up of different sections built with a variety of materials over the centuries. Much of it was not a “wall” as your kids would envision it, but rather, mounds of dirt or stones. Not much of the early sections are still standing today. They largely have eroded away or have been scrapped for building materials.

What you are mostly likely to see during your visit is the section built during the Ming dynasty, which ruled China from 1368 to 1644. The brick structure is most of what remains today, and it certainly is the most visually stunning.

After the Ming dynasty, no additional construction of the Great Wall, at least not as a defensive structure, took place. However, some restoration work has been done to make it more attractive to visitors. Even so, sections of it are still being discovered today. In 2012, researchers found a “lost” section in the Gobi Desert across the border in Mongolia, stretching about 62 miles in length.

Explaining the Magnitude

The “official” Great Wall of China length is about 13,000 miles. You can help your kids visualize that by explaining it’s only a little less than the distance from New York to Beijing and back. That length takes into account all the various sections of the wall that were built across centuries.

The most modern section built during the Ming dynasty spans about 5,500 miles in total. That includes portions of natural defenses, such as rivers and hills, as well as the synthetic portions.
