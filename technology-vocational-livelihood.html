2. "examining the challenges faced by technical vocational livelihood students in first Academy skills and technology "​


Explanation:

I can provide an overview of the challenges that technical vocational livelihood students face in First Academy Skills and Technology, based on research studies and relevant literature. The following are some of the common challenges faced by technical vocational livelihood students:

1. Limited resources and facilities: Technical vocational education requires specialized equipment and facilities, such as laboratories, workshops, and advanced technology devices. However, many institutions in developing countries, especially those in rural areas, lack adequate resources and infrastructure to provide quality technical education. This could limit the exposure of students to practical skills and reduce their chances of performing well in their chosen profession.

2. Inadequate training: Technical vocational education is experiential and practical by nature. However, in some institutions, trainers may lack relevant industry experience, leading to the delivery of theoretical knowledge with little practical application. This could result in producing graduates who are not adequately equipped with the skills and knowledge required in the industry.

3. Limited job opportunities: In some countries, technical and vocational skills are not adequately recognized, leading to a lack of job opportunities for graduates. Employers may have a preference for graduates from traditional academic programs, such as medicine and engineering, resulting in the lack of recognition of technical and vocational skills.

4. Negative social stigma: There is a social perception that technical and vocational education is for individuals who are not academically bright, leading to a negative stigma around the profession. This perception discourages talented individuals from pursuing vocational education, leading to a limited pool of skilled professionals.

5. Inability to keep up with current industry trends: Technical and vocational education programs require regular updates to ensure that the curriculum is relevant to the current industry trends. However, some institutions may not be able to keep up with the fast-changing industry, leading to outdated curriculum that does not match with the current demands of the industry.

In conclusion, technical vocational livelihood students face several challenges that could limit their success in the industry. Institutions and governments need to address these challenges to ensure that students receive quality technical education that equips them with the required skills for employment and entrepreneurship.

16. where did start technical vocational livelihood ?​


Answer:

Technical-vocational education and training (TVET) has a long history, with roots that can be traced back to the Middle Ages when apprenticeships were used to teach young people various trades and crafts. In modern times, the idea of combining technical skills with vocational education gained popularity in the 19th and early 20th centuries, especially in Germany, where the "Dual System" of education was developed. This system combined classroom-based education with on-the-job training, providing students with the practical skills and knowledge needed to succeed in the workforce.

TVET programs have since spread to other countries and are now a vital part of the educational systems in many regions around the world, including Asia, Africa, and Latin America. The goal of TVET is to provide individuals with the skills and knowledge needed to enter the workforce and to support their personal and professional development.
